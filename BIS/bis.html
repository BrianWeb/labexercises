Big Data Case Study – Target

Company example: Target
  • Target analysed millions of customer transactions.
  • They discovered certain items were frequently purchased together by pregnant women.
  • Products such as unscented lotion, vitamins and cotton balls formed a pattern.
  • Using this pattern, Target built a prediction model.
  • They began sending baby product coupons to customers before competitors knew they were expecting.
Why it’s memorable
  • One father complained that his teenage daughter received baby coupons.
  • It turned out she was pregnant — and Target’s data had predicted it.
  • Big Data Insight: it wasn’t one purchase that mattered — it was the pattern.
Teaching prompt: “Which is more powerful: knowing one fact, or spotting a pattern across thousands of facts?”

From Data to Knowledge (Retail Example)

Concept: Data → Information → Knowledge → Action
Data
  • Product IDs
  • Customer IDs
  • Date & time of purchase
  • Quantity
Information
  • Customer A buys nappies and baby wipes weekly.
  • Customer B buys protein bars every Monday.
Knowledge
  • Customer A likely has a baby.
  • Customer B likely attends the gym regularly.
  • Certain products are often bought together.
Business Action
  • Targeted promotions
  • Cross-selling
  • Personalised marketing
  • Increased sales
Key line: “Big Data creates value when it changes decisions and behaviour.”

Netflix vs Blockbuster – Data Wins

Company example: Netflix vs Blockbuster
  • Blockbuster relied on physical stores and late fees.
  • Netflix collected data on what customers watched, paused, re-watched and rated.
  • Netflix analysed viewing patterns across millions of users.
  • They recommended shows based on user behaviour.
  • They even created original shows based on viewing data.
Result
  • Blockbuster filed for bankruptcy.
  • Netflix became a global streaming giant.
  • Competitive advantage came from data insight — not store size.
Class question: “Blockbuster had stores. Netflix had data. Which is easier to scale globally?”

Big Data in Everyday Life – Google Maps

Company example: Google
  • Google Maps does not use satellites to detect traffic jams.
  • It measures how fast phones using Maps are moving.
  • If many phones slow down in one location, it detects traffic.
  • It predicts arrival times based on millions of previous journeys.
  • Even if only 1% of drivers use Maps, that can still be thousands of data points daily.
Big Data allows
  • Real-time decision making
  • Accurate predictions
  • Continuous improvement
  • The more data collected, the smarter the system becomes
Quick analogy: “Your phone becomes a sensor. Millions of sensors become a prediction engine.”

The 4th V – Value

Extension: beyond Volume, Velocity, Variety
  • Volume – large amounts of data
  • Velocity – speed of data generation
  • Variety – different formats of data
  • Veracity – accuracy and reliability
Value – the most important V
  • Data has no value unless it improves decisions.
  • Big Data is not about size — it is about smarter action.
  • The companies that extract value win.
One-liner for first years: “Storage is not strategy. Decisions are strategy.”

Discussion slide (works well for first year)

  • Is targeted advertising helpful or intrusive?
  • Should customers have more control over their data?
  • How does GDPR affect Big Data in Europe?
