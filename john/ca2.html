2) High-2.1 / First-class model answer (Irish solicitor/lecturer style) ⌄

Introduction

Sheila seeks advice as to whether an agreement she entered into with her 15-year-old nephew, Ted, at a family wedding is legally enforceable. She promised to buy Ted a car worth €20,000 if he abstained from alcohol, smoking, and other intoxicating substances until his eighteenth birthday. Ted claims he complied fully and now seeks performance. Sheila contends that the arrangement was a family understanding, never intended to have legal effect.

The key issues are: (1) whether the agreement was intended to create legal relations despite its family context; (2) whether Ted provided valid consideration; and (3) the effect of Ted’s minority on enforceability under Irish law.

Intention to Create Legal Relations

Intention to create legal relations is an essential requirement for contractual enforceability. While commercial agreements benefit from a rebuttable presumption of legal intention, family and social arrangements are presumed not to be legally binding. This presumption is exemplified by Balfour v Balfour [1919] 2 KB 571, where a domestic agreement was held not to give rise to legal obligations, and by Lens v Devonshire Club [1914], where a social promise was treated as unenforceable. By contrast, Merritt v Merritt [1970] 1 WLR 1211 illustrates that the presumption may be rebutted where surrounding circumstances clearly demonstrate legal intention.

Here, the agreement was made in a plainly familial, social setting (a wedding) between an aunt and nephew. There is no commercial context, no legal advice, and no indication that either party discussed legal consequences at the time. Although the promise was reduced to writing and signed, this alone is not necessarily sufficient to rebut the presumption against legal intention in family arrangements, particularly where the apparent purpose is encouragement and moral guidance rather than legal commitment.

Accordingly, Sheila has a strong argument that the arrangement lacked the requisite intention to create legal relations.

Consideration

Consideration requires that the promisee confer a benefit on the promisor or incur a detriment at the promisor’s request. Forbearance may constitute valid consideration (see Currie v Misa (1875) LR 10 Ex 153). In Hamer v Sidway (1891) 124 NY 538 (a US authority and therefore persuasive only), the court treated a nephew’s forbearance from drinking and smoking as good consideration for his uncle’s promise.

On these facts, Ted’s abstinence could arguably amount to consideration. However, the argument is weakened because Ted was already legally restricted from some of the relevant conduct for part of the period due to his age. While full abstinence may go beyond statutory restrictions, its “value” as consideration is not wholly straightforward. In any event, even if consideration exists, enforceability still depends on intention and capacity.

Capacity and Contracts with Minors

The most significant issue is Ted’s age at formation. Under Irish law, minors have limited contractual capacity. Contracts for necessaries or those clearly for the minor’s benefit may be binding, but agreements falling outside these categories are generally not enforceable against a minor and, crucially, are not readily enforceable by a minor against an adult where policy requires the minor’s protection.

The protective rationale is illustrated in Leslie Ltd v Sheill [1914] 3 KB 607, where the court refused to enforce contractual remedies against a minor despite misrepresentation, reflecting the strong policy of protecting minors from the consequences of improvident agreements. In this case, the agreement is not one for necessaries, nor is it essential to Ted’s maintenance or education. While Ted may argue that the agreement was beneficial and fully performed, performance and understanding do not, of themselves, overcome the protective rules governing minors’ capacity.

This point strongly supports Sheila: even if the arrangement looked like a bargain, Ted’s minority at the time of contracting presents a substantial barrier to enforcement.

Certainty and Formality

The terms are reasonably clear: abstinence until 18 in exchange for a car worth €20,000. However, certainty alone does not cure the absence of intention or capacity. No solicitor’s witnessing is required for a simple contract, but the informal setting and family context reinforce the view that this was a moral or social promise rather than a legally enforceable obligation.

Conclusion and Advice

On balance, Sheila is unlikely to be legally obliged to provide the car. The agreement is best characterised as a family arrangement lacking intention to create legal relations. Even if intention and consideration could be argued, Ted’s minority at formation creates a further and significant obstacle to enforceability. Sheila can therefore be advised that she does not owe Ted a car in law, notwithstanding that Ted may feel morally aggrieved.
